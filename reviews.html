Daisy Jones and The Six

Taylor Jenkins Reid
March 4th, 2020

Daisy Jones is a neglected teenager from a rich family that turns to drugs and bad men in her endless quest for attention, while in possession of an unparalleled musical talent that could make everything alright, if only she was properly heard. Billy Dunne is a sensitive, attractive young man, son to an absent father, who pours himself into music. Well if that sounded a little trite to you, that makes two of us. Add to that Billy’s less-talented-more-likeable brother, Daisy’s selfless BFF, the band’s unrecognised keyboardist, a producer convinced of these people’s potential and capable of managing everyone’s egos, plus a cohort of unimportant group members, with more or less plain personalities. Now, spice everything up by name-dropping some drugs and throw in a couple of sex stories, cook for some 400 pages and you’ll have something very similar to Daisy Jones & The Six.

Don’t get me wrong, the book undeniably works; it makes for an engaging read and a few cackles. However, the plot unfolded rather predictably, except for the identity of the narrator, which I just didn’t care for; and the characters felt mostly irreflexive, or were simply too inarticulate, about their own actions, even from the vantage point of what would have been a couple decades’ hindsight. Notable exceptions could be Camila’s and Karen’s stories, as well as Billy’s struggle with addiction; but all three are truly only touched upon, discussed only superficially, and left for the reader to fill in the narrative blanks with what, at least for the least informed of us, will inevitably be more clichés.

All in all, the story between Billy and Daisy was quite touching and one can only wish their music existed, but for the Goodreads Choice Winner in Historical Fiction 2019, this was just not particularly memorable.

A Confederacy of Dunces

John Kennedy Toole
February 14th, 2020

Even though the out-loud laughter that is promised in this book's numerous reviews eluded me, I did find myself grinning at it, and even conceded the occasional snort. However, some of the humour has aged quite drastically since the 1960's, and I couldn't help but feel that the lives of so many of the characters were actually tragic, which spoiled part of the fun.

The New York Trilogy

Paul Auster
January 13th, 2020

As the title suggest, The New York trilogy is a collection of three short books that are commonly published together but were in fact written on separate years by American author Paul Auster. While chracters and stories change from one story to the next, all three are about detectives and have the themes of identity and reality as leit motiv. Charicaturesque and weird at times, this is not a book for everyone.
